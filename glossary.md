# Glossary

This glossary supports a limited conceptual framework for describing intermediate phenomena in advanced language systems. It is not intended as a theory of consciousness, personhood, or full agency. Its purpose is narrower: to name organized patterns that may be practically real without thereby becoming sovereign.

## Appearance

The initial impression of coherence, directedness, style, or point of view.  
Appearance is the weakest threshold. It marks how a system seems, not yet what standing its behavior deserves.

## Presence

A felt continuity across an exchange.  
Presence begins when coherence is not merely decorative or momentary, but seems to remain across turns or recursive use.

## Tenue

A local holding capacity.  
Tenue names the ability of a system to keep a line together: to preserve orientation, maintain a thematic center, or hold local form across recursive interaction. English has no exact equivalent; “holding form” or “staying power” are only approximations.

## Efficacy

The capacity of an output to make a real difference in practice.  
A system is efficacious when its output improves a draft, stabilizes a workflow, sharpens reasoning, compresses state, or otherwise alters what happens next in a meaningful way.

## Legitimacy

Bounded recognized standing within a context.  
Legitimacy does not mean full authority. It means that a system or output is accepted as valid, useful, or appropriate within a limited corridor such as drafting, review, triage, or preparation.

## Sovereignty

Final or supreme authority within a jurisdiction.  
A sovereign form does not merely influence; it settles. It does not merely persist; it governs. Sovereignty implies a power to close, decide, or authorize without depending on constant outside renewal.

## Non-sovereign form

An organized pattern that is real enough to persist, influence, or support practice, but does not possess final authority over its own status, closure, or continuation.  
This is the central term of the note.

## Closure

A settlement that counts for what follows.  
Closure is stronger than a response ending cleanly. In the stronger sense used here, closure means that a sequence becomes normatively or operationally settled.

## Apparent closure

The performance or language of finality without real settlement.  
A system may sound conclusive while leaving the underlying issue effectively unresolved.

## Effective closure

A closure that actually governs what follows.  
An effective closure changes the status of the sequence, not just its tone.

## Jurisdiction

The bounded domain within which an output may bear force.  
A system may have drafting jurisdiction without having permission jurisdiction. It may summarize state without being authorized to write canonical state.

## Re-injection

The renewed feeding of a system through prompts, recap scaffolds, memory retrieval, verifier structures, or repeated human endorsement.  
Re-injection often explains why a system can sustain local quality or revision without possessing self-grounded closure.

## Probabilistic coherence

A pattern’s stability, plausibility, or recurrence within generation.  
A form may be highly coherent in this sense without having any right to rule or settle anything.

## Normative coherence

A pattern’s standing to organize what should follow.  
Normative coherence is stronger than probabilistic coherence. It concerns authority, not just stability.

## Pseudo-reflection

Behavior that structurally resembles reflection while remaining dependent on externalized renewal conditions.  
Pseudo-reflection should not be read as “mere illusion” in a dismissive sense. The loop may be real and useful, while still falling short of sovereign self-grounding.

## Local stability

The persistence of a pattern across turns, revisions, or recursive use within a bounded interaction.  
Local stability matters because many practically relevant forms do not need global unity to become influential.

## Canonical state

A stronger external source of truth, record, or authority against which a system’s output can be tested.  
Examples include repositories, governed documents, verifier systems, or authoritative records.

## Contested closure

A situation in which a system’s own framing of settlement is tested against a stronger external source.  
This is useful diagnostically: if closure dissolves immediately under contestation, it was likely only apparent.

## Delegated role

A limited function granted to a system within a workflow without transferring sovereignty to it.  
Examples include drafting, reviewing, summarizing, or preparing gates for human decision.

## Reflective loop

A recursive sequence in which a system reuses its own prior outputs as fresh context for revision, critique, or correction.  
Reflective loops may increase efficacy without proving self-grounding.

## Middle zone

The conceptual territory between trivial surface generation and stronger claims such as consciousness, full agency, or sovereignty.  
This glossary is meant to clarify that zone, not to abolish it.
